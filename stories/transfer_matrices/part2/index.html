    Tacos and Burgers

    You are at a carnival, it is 40C degrees outside and your are hungry. There are only two good food stands in the carnival, tacos and burgers. The two stands are right next to each other, so essentially they share a single line people.

    • Each person in the carnival wants either a taco or a burger.
    • When a person reaches the front of the line, he goes for the stand he wanted, orders, and waits until his meal is ready.
    • While that person waits, the entire line stands still and waits for him to get his food (very annoying, I know). That is unless the person right behind him wants the other stand, and then they both wait together.
    • The preperation time for a pizza or a burger is identical, and it is exactly one minute.
    • exactly half of the people in the line want pizzas, and the other half want burgers.
    • You are the 1000th person in line, how long are you expected to wait?

    Solution

    Let's say that at some given moment, a person just got his food at one (or two persons got their food from both) of the stands. At that moment the first and second people waiting in line can have one of four different desires (respectively):

    • \(TT\) - Taco-Taco
    • \(BT\) - Burger-Taco
    • \(TB\) - Taco-Burger
    • \(BB\) - Burger-Burger

    At the next moment, a person (or two persons if we are lucky) goes to order in the stand(s). That will lead to a new pair of people at the front of the line (It is possible that the "second" person just got ahead one step and is the new "first" person), again, there are only four options for those pair. The question asked is given the initial state, what is the probability of getting each of the other states in the next cycle. The following table encodes this data exactly.

    \ \(BB\) \(BT\) \(TB\) \(TT\)
    \(BB\) \(\frac{1}{2}\) \(\frac{1}{4}\) \(\frac{1}{4}\) \(0\)
    \(BT\) \(0\) \(\frac{1}{4}\) \(\frac{1}{4}\) \(\frac{1}{2}\)
    \(TB\) \(\frac{1}{2}\) \(\frac{1}{4}\) \(\frac{1}{4}\) \(0\)
    \(TT\) \(0\) \(\frac{1}{4}\) \(\frac{1}{4}\) \(\frac{1}{2}\)

    From reasons that will become clear soon (TODO(gilro)), we reformulate the states as:

    • \(S\) - same state, either \(BB\) or \(TT\)
    • \(M\) - mixed state, either \(BT\) or \(TB\)
    \ \(S\) \(M\)
    \(S\) \(\frac{1}{2}\) \(\frac{1}{2}\)
    \(M\) \(\frac{1}{2}\) \(\frac{1}{2}\)

    Now the question at hand is the following:

    How much time do we spend on each state?

    Well, we start at some state, it can be either \(S\) or \(M\). The table above shows that we have a probability of \(\frac{1}{2}\) to stay in the same state and a probability of \(\frac{1}{2}\) to transition to the other state. Now it's time for an important definition: The state vector.

    \[\vec{s}=\begin{bmatrix} P(S) \cr P(M) \end{bmatrix}\]

    The state vector's entries denote the probability that the system is at a given state. \(P(S)\) is the probability that the first two people have the same food preferences, \(P(M)\) is the probability that they have different preferences (note that they always sum to \(1\)). For example, if initially the two first people standing in line are both taco lovers, the initial state vector is:

    \[\vec{s_{init}}=\begin{bmatrix} 1 \cr 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

    After the first one went to get his taco, there is a 50% probability that the first two people at the head of the line are both taco lovers, and a 50% chance that the second is a burger lover. Therefore the state vector after the first turn is:

    \[\vec{s_{1}}=\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \cr \frac{1}{2} \end{bmatrix}\]

    How about the next iterations? TODO(explain better), is pretty easy to see that forever after the state will remain:

    \[\vec{s_{n}}=\begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \cr \frac{1}{2} \end{bmatrix}\]

    Suppose now that you have two cousins, a smart one and a less brighter one (don't worry he has a great personality and he is very good at football). They give you two interpretations:

    • "Half of the people people await for their food while the other stand is empty."
    • "Half of the time both stands are working, while in the other half only one of them is working.

    Which of the statements was given by our football loving cousin? well, it is the first one that is wrong, since whenever both stands are operating, they involve two people waiting simulateously. The \(M\) states make the line go twice as faster as the \(S\) states.

    Now, since half of the turns (the \(S\) states) go at a rate of 1 person per minute, and the other half, the \(M\) states go at a rate of two persons per minute, the combined rate is:

    \[ R = \frac{1}{2} + 1 = \frac{3}{2} \]

    persons per minute. So our final answer is that you are expected to wait approximately \(1000*3/2 = 1500\) minutes in line (25 hours). Good luck.

    Generalizing

    Now let's say that \(x\) percent of the people in line like pizzas, while the other \(1-x\) percent like burgers (in the previous example \(x=\frac{1}{2}\)). Let's rewrite the transition probabilities.

    \ \(BB\) \(BT\) \(TB\) \(TT\)
    \(BB\) \(1-x\) \((1-x)^2\) \((1-x)^2\) \(0\)
    \(BT\) \(0\) \(x(1-x)\) \(x(1-x)\) \(1-x\)
    \(TB\) \(x\) \(x(1-x)\) \(x(1-x)\) \(0\)
    \(TT\) \(0\) \(x^2\) \(x^2\) \(x\)

    Now we denote:

    • \(T\) - taco state, \(TT\).
    • \(B\) - burger state, \(BB\).
    • \(M\) - mixed state, either \(BT\) or \(TB\).
    \ \(B\) \(M\) \(T\)
    \(B\) \(1-x\) \((1-x)^2\) \(0\)
    \(M\) \(x\) \(2x(1-x)\) \(1-x\)
    \(T\) \(0\) \(x^2\) \(x\)

    The steady state vector corresponding to this transition matrix is:

    \[ \vec{v}=\begin{bmatrix} \frac{(1-x)^3}{1-2x+2x^2} \cr \frac{x(1-x)}{1-2x+2x^2} \cr \frac{x^3}{1-2x+2x^2} \end{bmatrix}\]

    and indeed for \(x=\frac{1}{2}\) we recover \(\vec{v}=(\frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{4})\)

    • The taco stand is idle \(\frac{(1-x)^3}{1-2x+2x^2}\) of the time.
    • The burger stand is idle \(\frac{x^3}{1-2x+2x^2}\) of the time.
    • The combined rate is :
    \[ R = people/time = \frac{n_B \cdot 1+ n_H \cdot 1 + n_M \cdot 2}{n_B + n_H + n_M}\]
    \[ = P(B) + P(T) + 2P(M) \]
    \[ = \frac{(1-x)^3}{1-2x+2x^2} + \frac{x^3}{1-2x+2x^2} + \frac{2x(1-x)}{1-2x+2x^2}\]
    \[ = \frac{(1-x)^3+x^3+2x(1-x)}{1-2x+2x^2} = \frac{1-x+x^2}{1-2x+2x^2} \]
    \[ = \frac{(x-\frac{1}{2})^2 + \frac{3}{4}}{2(x-\frac{1}{2})^2 + \frac{1}{2}} \]
